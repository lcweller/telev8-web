# StoryBrand Framework — Reference Guide for Brand Creation

*A working reference summarizing the SB7 Framework from Donald Miller's "Building a StoryBrand." Written in my own words as an instructional guide. Intended as context for an AI assistant tasked with creating brand messaging, websites, taglines, email campaigns, and marketing copy.*

---

## How to Use This Document

When asked to create or refine a brand, work through the seven framework elements in order. Each element has (a) the underlying principle, (b) the rules and pitfalls, and (c) worksheet-style prompts to extract the right answers from the user. The BrandScript at the end is the master deliverable — every other asset (homepage, lead magnet, email sequence, one-liner) should derive from it.

**The cardinal rule:** the customer is the hero of the story. The brand is the guide. Any copy that violates this — copy that makes the brand the protagonist — should be rewritten.

---

## Foundational Principles

### Why Story Works
The human brain is wired to (1) survive and thrive, and (2) conserve calories. Marketing copy that is unclear, self-focused, or cluttered forces the brain to burn energy decoding it — so the brain tunes out. Story is the most efficient information-delivery format the brain knows. Apply story structure to messaging and comprehension goes up dramatically.

### The Two Questions
Within seconds of encountering a brand, every customer is silently asking:
1. **What do you offer?**
2. **How will it make my life better?**
3. *(And implicitly: what do I need to do to get it?)*

If the homepage, email subject line, or ad doesn't answer these instantly, the customer leaves.

### The Grunt Test
A useful sanity check: a caveman looking at the home page should be able to grunt out three things within five seconds:
- What you offer
- How it'll make their life better
- What they need to do to buy it

If the answer to any of these requires interpretation, the messaging fails.

### Clarity Over Cleverness
Cleverness, wordplay, and brand poetry routinely sabotage conversion. Clear and slightly boring beats clever and ambiguous every time. "If you confuse, you lose."

## The SB7 Framework — The Master Sentence

> A **CHARACTER** has a **PROBLEM** and meets a **GUIDE** who gives them a **PLAN** and **CALLS THEM TO ACTION** that ends in **SUCCESS** and helps them avoid **FAILURE**.

This is the spine. Memorize it. Every brand asset should map cleanly onto these seven beats.

---

## Element 1: A Character (The Customer Is the Hero)

### Principle
The customer is Luke Skywalker; the brand is Yoda. The most common branding mistake is positioning the company as the hero — talking about its history, its values, its founder's vision, its team's credentials. Customers don't care about the brand's story. They care about their own.

### Rules
- **Define one clear desire** the customer has, in seven words or fewer.
- **The desire must be relevant to what you sell.** "We help you live your best life" fails — too vague, not tied to a product.
- **One desire only.** The brain can only track one story goal at a time. Multiple goals = no goal.
- The desire should connect to a primal aspiration: financial security, status, freedom, generosity, meaningful relationships, hope of meaning, physical health, or peace of mind.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. In seven words or fewer, what does your customer want as it relates to your brand?
2. Why do they want that? (Use this to inform the internal-problem layer later.)

### Output Format Example
> *Customer desire: "A reliable monthly retainer of new clients."*

---

## Element 2: Has a Problem

### Principle
Conflict is the hook. The clearer you articulate the customer's problem, the more they feel understood — and people buy from brands that understand them. Problems exist on three layers, and great messaging speaks to all three.

### The Three Levels of Problem

**External Problem** — The tangible, surface-level issue. The thing visibly wrong. A leaky faucet. An overgrown lawn. A confusing tax situation.

**Internal Problem** — The frustration, self-doubt, or emotion the external problem produces. This is the layer most brands skip, and it's the one that drives the actual decision to buy. Customers buy products to resolve internal problems. *Example: the leaky faucet creates the feeling of "I'm not on top of my home."*

**Philosophical Problem** — The bigger "why it matters in the universe" frame. Your product becomes part of a story about right and wrong, justice, dignity. *Example: "Homeowners shouldn't have to feel ashamed of their homes."*

### The Villain
Personify the source of the problem. The villain doesn't have to be a person — it can be a force, a system, a condition. Rules for a good villain:
- **Root** — the deep cause, not a surface symptom
- **Relatable** — customers should recognize it from their own experience
- **Singular** — one villain, not five
- **Real** — not melodramatic; should pass the laugh test

*Examples:* tax confusion, junk food, cluttered closets, slow Mondays, the inbox.

### Rules
- The internal problem is your highest-leverage messaging layer. Use it in headlines.
- Reference all three layers somewhere on the homepage.
- The villain should appear in headlines, ad copy, and email subject lines.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. Who or what is the villain in your customer's story?
2. What's the **external** problem you solve?
3. What **internal** frustration does that external problem create?
4. What **philosophical** injustice is at stake — what's "wrong with the world" that your product helps fix?

## Element 3: And Meets a Guide

### Principle
Customers aren't looking for another hero — they're looking for a guide who's been where they are and can show the way forward. Guides earn trust by demonstrating two things: empathy and authority.

### Empathy
A simple, sincere expression that you understand the customer's struggle.

Common opening phrases:
- "We understand how it feels to ___."
- "Nobody should have to ___."
- "Like you, we believe ___."

Empathy must be specific. Generic empathy ("we care about our customers") rings hollow. Specific empathy ("we know how exhausting it is to wake up at 3 AM worrying about cash flow") lands.

### Authority
Demonstrated competence. Not bragging — proof. Four primary forms:

1. **Testimonials** — let customers do the boasting. The single most powerful authority signal.
2. **Statistics** — clients served, dollars saved, problems solved, years in operation.
3. **Awards** — only if recognizable to the target audience; otherwise omit.
4. **Logos** — recognizable brands you've worked with or that have featured you.

### The Balance
Show enough authority to be credible. Too much and you stop being a guide and start being a hero again. The rule: ~10% authority, ~90% empathy + plan + offer.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. Write a one- or two-sentence empathy statement that names a specific frustration of your customer.
2. List three pieces of authority you can display (pick from testimonials, stats, awards, logos).

## Element 4: Who Gives Them a Plan

### Principle
The customer needs a path. A clearly articulated plan reduces the perceived complexity and risk of buying. The brain is happy to follow stepping stones; it refuses to ford rivers.

### Two Plan Types

**Process Plan** — A 3–4 step path showing how the customer does business with you (or how the product works). Three steps feels light; four feels solid; five or more feels overwhelming.

*Template:*
1. Step one (an easy first action).
2. Step two (the work happens here).
3. Step three (the customer enjoys the result).

*Example for a financial advisor:*
1. Schedule a free planning call.
2. We build your customized plan.
3. You retire with confidence.

**Agreement Plan** — A list of promises that addresses the customer's specific fears about doing business with you.

*Examples:*
- "No long-term contracts."
- "Same technician every visit."
- "100% money-back guarantee."
- "We'll never share your data."

### Rules
- Give each plan step a memorable name (not just "Step 1").
- Use both plan types when appropriate — process above the fold, agreement further down.
- The first step of the process should match your direct call to action exactly.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. What 3–4 step process gets a customer from "I'm interested" to "problem solved"?
2. What 3–5 promises would dissolve the most common objections?

## Element 5: And Calls Them to Action

### Principle
Heroes don't act unless they're challenged to. Customers won't act unless you ask them to — clearly, repeatedly, and confidently. Most websites are too passive: full of information, light on asks.

### Two Types of CTA

**Direct Calls to Action** — The actual ask. The commitment.

Examples:
- "Buy Now"
- "Schedule a Call"
- "Get Started"
- "Register Today"

Rules:
- Place in the top-right of every page (the eye lands there last and tends to settle there).
- Repeat throughout the page — homepage hero, mid-page, footer, and after each major section.
- Use the **same wording** every time. Don't get creative with synonyms.
- Avoid soft language: "Submit," "Learn More," "Click Here." These don't ask for commitment.

**Transitional Calls to Action** — Lower-commitment offers for customers who aren't ready to buy.

Examples:
- A free PDF guide in exchange for email
- A free webinar or workshop
- A free quiz, calculator, or assessment
- A free trial, sample, or consultation

Transitional CTAs do three things:
1. Reciprocity — you give first, they feel inclined to give back.
2. Positioning — you become the generous expert.
3. Capture — you collect the email and can stay in touch.

### Rules
- Always have one primary direct CTA per page.
- Always have one transitional CTA visible above the fold.
- Direct CTAs are buttons in a contrasting color. Transitional CTAs can be buttons or inline links.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. What's the single direct CTA for this brand?
2. What's a transitional CTA — a free resource that genuinely helps the customer and demonstrates expertise?

---

## Element 6: That Helps Them Avoid Failure

### Principle
Stakes. Without something at risk, no one cares how the story ends. Hint at what the customer stands to lose by not acting — but lightly. The "salt principle": just enough to flavor, never the whole shaker.

### The Stakes Layers
What does the customer's life look like if they *don't* choose your brand?

Common categories:
- Wasted money on the wrong solution
- Lost time
- Lost relationships, status, or reputation
- Continued physical or emotional pain
- A future that looks just like the unsatisfying present
- Embarrassment or shame
- Missed opportunity / passed-over moment

### Rules
- 3–4 specific costs of inaction is the right amount.
- Be concrete — "you'll keep losing $X per month" beats "things might not improve."
- Don't lead with fear. Use stakes as a brief contrast against the success picture.

### Worksheet Prompts
1. What 3–4 specific things will the customer's life keep looking like if they don't choose this brand?
2. What's the worst-case continued state? (Useful for one strong stakes line.)

---

## Element 7: And Ends in Success

### Principle
Show the customer what life looks like *after* your product solves their problem. Vivid, specific, sensory. Vague success language ("achieve your dreams") doesn't move anyone. Concrete after-pictures do.

### Three Universal Success Themes

**Status** — Belonging, prestige, recognition, becoming the kind of person you want to be seen as. *"Walk into the room and have everyone notice the suit fits perfectly."*

**Completion / Wholeness** — Filling a missing piece. Becoming who you were meant to be. Ending a long-running incompleteness.

**Self-Realization / Acceptance** — Inner peace. The internal problem is finally resolved. The recurring frustration is gone for good.

### Rules
- Use vivid, sensory language. Show, don't tell.
- Use images on the homepage that depict the after-state, not the before-state.
- Customer testimonials that describe the after-state are gold.
- Pick the success theme that matches the customer's deepest motivation (status for luxury goods; self-realization for therapy; completion for products that round out an identity).

### Worksheet Prompts
1. Describe the customer's life in vivid detail after your product has done its work. What do they see, feel, hear, do?
2. Which success theme dominates — status, completion, or self-realization?

## The Character Transformation Arc

Underneath the seven beats is the deeper engine of story: **the hero changes.** They start the story as one kind of person and end as another — braver, freer, more competent, more whole.

The brand's job is to:
1. Identify the **aspirational identity** the customer is reaching toward.
2. Name it.
3. Reflect it back to them in the messaging.

*Examples:*
- Runner → Athlete
- Saver → Wise Steward
- Hobbyist photographer → Visual Storyteller
- Stressed parent → Calm, Present Parent

When customers feel a brand is helping them become who they want to be, they don't just buy — they evangelize.

### Worksheet Prompt
In three words or fewer, who does your customer want to become?

## The BrandScript (Master Deliverable)

The BrandScript is the seven-element summary that drives all messaging. It looks like this:

```
CHARACTER:
  What does the customer want?

PROBLEM:
  Villain:
  External:
  Internal:
  Philosophical:

GUIDE:
  Empathy statement:
  Authority (3 proof points):

PLAN:
  Process plan (3–4 steps):
  Agreement plan (3–5 promises):

CALL TO ACTION:
  Direct:
  Transitional:

FAILURE:
  3–4 things to avoid:

SUCCESS:
  After-picture (vivid):

CHARACTER TRANSFORMATION:
  From: [old identity]
  To: [aspirational identity]
```

Every other brand asset — website, email, ad, landing page, sales script, one-liner — pulls from this document.

## The One-Liner

A One-Liner is the elevator pitch derived from the BrandScript. Used at networking events, in email signatures, on social profiles, and as a homepage tagline candidate.

### Formula
**Customer's problem + product/solution + result/transformation**

### Examples
- *"Most homeowners are nervous about choosing a contractor. We give you a fixed-price, fixed-timeline guarantee — so you can renovate without the dread."*
- *"Most small business owners feel overwhelmed by their finances. We provide a monthly bookkeeping service that hands you clean numbers every Friday — so you always know where you stand."*

### Rules
- Start with the problem, not the brand.
- Be concrete about the result.
- Read it out loud. If it sounds like marketing speak, rewrite it as plain conversation.

## Applying the Framework

### Homepage Wireframe (Above the Fold)
1. **Headline** — Speaks to the customer's external + internal problem AND hints at the success after-picture. Often pairs the offer with the transformation.
2. **Subheading** — Adds clarity on what you offer.
3. **Direct CTA button** — Top-right and inside the hero section.
4. **Transitional CTA** — Soft button next to the direct CTA.
5. **Hero image** — Shows the customer in the success after-state, smiling, using the product, or the result of the product. Avoid generic stock photos.

### Standard Homepage Section Order
1. Hero (above the fold, as above)
2. The Stakes (light — what they're losing by not acting)
3. Value Proposition (3 short benefit statements)
4. Guide section (empathy + authority — your "why us")
5. The Plan (3–4 step process)
6. Explanatory Paragraph (long-form — for the readers)
7. Video (optional but powerful)
8. Price/Tier comparison (if relevant)
9. Junk Drawer at the bottom (about, contact, careers — anything that isn't story-relevant)

### Lead Generators (Transitional CTAs)
The best lead generators:
- Solve a small, specific problem the customer has *right now*
- Establish expertise in your domain
- Take less than 10 minutes to consume

Format ideas:
- Downloadable PDF guide / checklist
- Quiz with personalized result
- Free webinar or workshop
- Free assessment / audit
- Free first session / sample

### Email Marketing
**Nurture sequence (post-lead-magnet):** 5–7 emails delivered weekly that:
1. Deliver the lead magnet + brief intro
2. Talk about a problem (the villain)
3. Tell a story of someone who solved it
4. Address an objection
5. Address another objection
6. Soft sales email with direct CTA
7. Hard sales email — the close

**Sales email sequence:** 5 emails over 5–7 days during a launch:
1. Deliver value (a tip, a story)
2. Articulate the problem and stakes
3. Explain the product and the plan
4. Address objections
5. Final call — deadline, scarcity, last chance

### Tagline / Headline Templates
- *"[Outcome] without [common pain point]."*
- *"The [adjective] way to [achieve desire]."*
- *"For [audience] who want to [aspiration]."*
- *"Stop [pain]. Start [success]."*

## Common Pitfalls (Audit Checklist)

When reviewing existing brand copy, flag any of the following:

- [ ] Brand is talking about itself in the hero section instead of the customer
- [ ] No clear answer to "what do you offer?" within 5 seconds
- [ ] No clear answer to "how will it make my life better?"
- [ ] No direct CTA visible above the fold
- [ ] Direct CTA wording varies across the page
- [ ] No transitional CTA
- [ ] Only the external problem is named (internal and philosophical missing)
- [ ] No villain
- [ ] Empathy statement is generic ("we care") instead of specific
- [ ] Authority section is too long or too boastful
- [ ] No process plan
- [ ] Stakes section is missing or melodramatic
- [ ] Success after-picture is vague
- [ ] Headlines use clever wordplay over clarity
- [ ] Hero image is a generic stock photo unrelated to the success state
- [ ] Customer's aspirational identity is not named anywhere

## Quick Reference: Element-to-Page-Element Mapping

| SB7 Element | Where It Shows Up |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Character (desire) | Headline, hero image, customer-facing language throughout |
| Problem | Subheadline, "the stakes" section, ad copy hooks |
| Guide | "About us" framed as empathy + authority; testimonial section |
| Plan | "How it works" section; process diagram |
| CTA | Header buttons, end-of-section buttons, footer |
| Failure | Stakes section; objection-handling paragraphs |
| Success | Hero image, after-pictures, testimonials, transformation language |

## Operating Instructions for the Receiving AI

When asked to build or revise a brand:

1. **Begin by extracting the BrandScript.** If the user hasn't filled it in, walk them through the seven elements one at a time using the worksheet prompts. Don't skip ahead.
2. **Validate the customer desire.** If it's longer than seven words or unrelated to the product, push back and ask the user to refine.
3. **Insist on all three problem layers.** Most users only volunteer the external problem. Probe for internal and philosophical.
4. **Demand specificity in empathy.** "We care about our customers" is not empathy. Rewrite it.
5. **Cap the process plan at 4 steps.** Combine or cut if longer.
6. **Lock the direct CTA wording.** Use it identically everywhere.
7. **Test every headline against the Grunt Test.** Could a caveman parse what's offered, the benefit, and the action?
8. **Default to clarity.** When in doubt between clever and clear, choose clear.
9. **Audit existing copy against the pitfalls checklist** before writing new copy.
10. **Derive every asset from the BrandScript.** If a piece of copy doesn't trace back to one of the seven elements, cut it or rewrite it.
